Week 11: Controlling for Confounders — The Frisch-Waugh-Lovell Theorem

Framing note: Last time we established that ML alone cannot answer causal questions. Today we build the mathematical foundation for what “controlling for confounders” actually means. This is not just a useful theorem — it is the engine that drives everything in weeks 12 and 15.

1. Motivation: What Does “Controlling For” Actually Mean?

  • In marketing we often want: what is the effect of ad spend on sales, holding everything else constant?
  • Common instinct: add controls to a regression and read off the coefficient
  • But why does this work? And when does it stop working?
  • FWL gives us the precise answer — and reveals the limits

2. The Setup: Full Regression Model

Consider the model: \[Y_i = \alpha + \beta T_i + \gamma' X_i + \epsilon_i\]

where: - \(Y_i\): outcome (e.g. monthly sales revenue, $000s) - \(T_i\): treatment / variable of interest (e.g. digital ad spend, $000s) - \(X_i\): controls / confounders (e.g. product quality score 1–10) - \(\beta\): the causal effect we want — effect of ad spend on sales, holding quality constant

Example estimates: \(\hat{\beta} = 1.5\) (£1k more ad spend → £1.5k more sales), \(\hat{\gamma} = 2.0\) (one unit quality → £2k more sales)

3. The FWL Theorem: Statement

Frisch-Waugh-Lovell Theorem: The OLS coefficient \(\hat{\beta}\) on \(T\) in the full regression can be recovered exactly by the following three-step procedure:

Step 1: Regress \(Y\) on \(X\), obtain residuals \(\tilde{Y}\) \[Y_i = \alpha_1 + \gamma' X_i + \tilde{Y}_i\]

Step 2: Regress \(T\) on \(X\), obtain residuals \(\tilde{T}\) \[T_i = \alpha_2 + \phi' X_i + \tilde{T}_i\]

Step 3: Regress \(\tilde{Y}\) on \(\tilde{T}\) (no intercept needed): \[\tilde{Y}_i = \beta \tilde{T}_i + \text{error}_i\]

The coefficient \(\hat{\beta}\) from Step 3 is identical to the one from the full regression.

4. Geometric Intuition: Partialling Out

  • Think of each variable as a vector in \(\mathbb{R}^n\)
  • Regressing \(Y\) on \(X\) and taking residuals = projecting \(Y\) onto the orthogonal complement of the column space of \(X\)
  • \(\tilde{Y}\) = the part of \(Y\) that \(X\) cannot explain
  • \(\tilde{T}\) = the part of \(T\) that \(X\) cannot explain
  • Regressing \(\tilde{Y}\) on \(\tilde{T}\): how does the unexplained variation in the outcome move with the unexplained variation in the treatment?
  • This is what “controlling for X” means geometrically: you are working in the space orthogonal to X

5. Marketing Example Walkthrough

Scenario: estimating the effect of digital ad spend on sales, controlling for product quality

Step What we do What we get
Step 1 Regress Sales on ProductQuality Residual sales: sales variation not explained by quality
Step 2 Regress AdSpend on ProductQuality Residual ad spend: ad variation not explained by quality
Step 3 Regress residual sales on residual ad spend \(\hat{\beta} = 1.5\) — pure effect of ad spend

Interpretation: £1k increase in ad spend (orthogonal to product quality) → £1.5k increase in sales. We have surgically removed the confounding influence of quality from both variables before estimating the relationship.

6. Why FWL Matters Beyond the Theorem

  • It tells us that \(\hat{\beta}\) in any OLS regression is always a partialling-out estimator
  • Every time you add controls to a regression, you are implicitly running FWL
  • This reframes the entire logic of causal estimation: it is about isolating variation in T that is exogenous to X
  • It also reveals the assumption: the residualisation in Steps 1 and 2 must be done correctly — if the relationship between \(T\) and \(X\) is non-linear and we use OLS, we haven’t fully partialled out \(X\)

7. Collider Bias Warning

  • FWL tells you what to partial out — it doesn’t tell you what to include in \(X\)
  • Confounder \(C\): causes both \(T\) and \(Y\) → must control for it
  • Collider \(L\): caused by both \(T\) and \(Y\) → must not control for it
  • Controlling for a collider opens a spurious path between \(T\) and \(Y\) — collider bias
  • Example: controlling for “website visits” when estimating ad spend → sales, if visits is caused by both ad spend and sales intentions
  • Use your DAG to determine the correct adjustment set before running anything

8. The High-Dimensional Problem: When OLS FWL Breaks

  • FWL works perfectly when \(p\) (number of controls) is small relative to \(n\)
  • What happens when \(p\) is large?
Problem What breaks
\(p > n\) OLS cannot be computed — \((X'X)\) is not invertible
\(p\) close to \(n\) OLS overfits the nuisance regressions in Steps 1 & 2
Many correlated controls Multicollinearity inflates variance, residuals are noisy
Non-linear confounders OLS residuals in Steps 1 & 2 still contain confounding
  • The residuals \(\tilde{Y}\) and \(\tilde{T}\) from OLS partialling are biased when the true relationship is non-linear or high-dimensional
  • Biased residuals → biased \(\hat{\beta}\) in Step 3

9. The Solution Preview: Replace OLS with ML in Steps 1 & 2

  • FWL says: partial out \(X\) from both \(Y\) and \(T\), then regress residuals
  • It does not say the partialling must be done with OLS
  • What if we use flexible ML models in Steps 1 and 2?
    • Random Forest, Gradient Boosting, LASSO, Neural Net — any learner that can approximate \(\mathbb{E}[Y \mid X]\) and \(\mathbb{E}[T \mid X]\) well
  • This is precisely what Double Machine Learning does
  • Next week: why you can’t just swap ML in naively — and what cross-fitting solves

10. Comparison: Estimation Approaches

Approach Controls for non-linearity Valid in high dimensions Inference valid
Naïve OLS (no controls) ✗ — Biased
OLS with linear controls ✗ ✗ Biased if non-linear
OLS + FWL (linear partialling) ✗ ✗ Correct if linear
DML (ML partialling + cross-fitting) ✓ ✓ ✓

11. Formal Proof Sketch (Optional / Advanced)

The OLS estimator in the full model is: \[\hat{\beta}_{OLS} = (T' M_X T)^{-1} T' M_X Y\]

where \(M_X = I - X(X'X)^{-1}X'\) is the annihilator matrix that projects onto the orthogonal complement of \(\text{col}(X)\).

Note that \(M_X T = \tilde{T}\) and \(M_X Y = \tilde{Y}\), so: \[\hat{\beta}_{OLS} = (\tilde{T}'\tilde{T})^{-1}\tilde{T}'\tilde{Y}\]

which is exactly the OLS coefficient from regressing \(\tilde{Y}\) on \(\tilde{T}\). QED.
